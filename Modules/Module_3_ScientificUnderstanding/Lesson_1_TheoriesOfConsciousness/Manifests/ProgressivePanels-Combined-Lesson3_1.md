# Progressive Panels Content: Theories of Consciousness: Four Frameworks for Understanding Altered States

## Panel: Core Theories of Consciousness: GWT and IIT

### Section: Why Theories Matter for Practitioners

<p>You might wonder why a practitioner needs to understand consciousness theories. The answer is practical. when a client describes an experience - a quieting of the inner voice, a dissolution of boundary between self and space, a feeling that time stopped - you need language and frameworks to work with what they're telling you.</p><p>Without a theoretical foundation, you're listening to experiences you can't interpret. With one, you can help clients contextualize what happened, make better session design decisions, and explain the science to skeptical colleagues with precision. The frameworks in this lesson are tools, not abstractions.</p>

*Trigger:* Global Workspace Theory

---

### Section: Global Workspace Theory

**Image Location:** ../assets/images/module3/global_workspace.webp

<p>Global Workspace Theory, proposed by cognitive neuroscientist Bernard Baars in 1988, describes consciousness as a kind of shared workspace in the brain. Most mental processing happens in specialized, local systems that operate in parallel and mostly below awareness. When information becomes significant enough, it gets broadcast into this global workspace, and that broadcast is what we experience as conscious awareness.</p><p>Think of it as the difference between background computation and something making it onto the screen.</p><p>For cyberdelic design. high-quality immersive VR experiences flood this workspace with novel, coherent content. When the workspace is occupied that comprehensively, ordinary self-referential content loses its broadcast priority. The inner narrator, habitual worries, looping thoughts - all get crowded out, not suppressed.</p>

*Trigger:* Integrated Information Theory

---

### Section: Integrated Information Theory

**Image Location:** ../assets/images/module3/integrated_information.webp

<p>Integrated Information Theory (IIT), developed by neuroscientist Giulio Tononi, proposes that consciousness is identical to integrated information, a property he quantifies as phi (the Greek letter). The more a system integrates information across its components rather than processing it in isolated modules, the higher its phi, and the richer its conscious experience.</p><p>This theory has a specific design implication for cyberdelics. experiences that achieve high coherence across multiple sensory channels, visual, auditory, haptic, and proprioceptive, all synchronized and mutually reinforcing, produce richer experiential states than experiences where channels operate independently.</p><p>One explanation that follows. the reason a well-designed cyberdelic session with synchronized spatial audio, meaningful visuals, and intentional breathwork feels more profound than VR with good graphics alone is that the degree of integrated information across the person's sensory system is genuinely higher.</p>

*Trigger:* The Honest Calibration

---

### Section: The Honest Calibration

<p>Both theories are working frameworks, not settled science. Global Workspace Theory has strong experimental support for many of its predictions. Integrated Information Theory is influential and mathematically rigorous, but remains contested. Some researchers find its implications counterintuitive and its empirical predictions difficult to test cleanly.</p><p>This is worth knowing because credible practitioners distinguish between what is established and what is theoretical. If a client or colleague asks whether IIT is proven, the accurate answer is. it's a significant and widely-discussed framework that researchers actively debate, not a replicated empirical finding.</p><p>Hold these theories with appropriate confidence. They are genuinely useful lenses, not certified facts.</p>

## Panel: Perception Models: Predictive Processing and Embodied Cognition

### Section: The Brain Doesn't Perceive Reality. It Predicts It.

**Image Location:** ../assets/images/module3/predictive_brain.webp

<p>Here is one of the most counterintuitive findings in modern neuroscience, and it's been replicated enough to state with confidence. your brain does not passively receive sensory information and assemble it into perception. It does the opposite. It constantly generates predictions about what should be arriving, and only updates when those predictions are wrong.</p><p>What you experience as 'seeing' or 'hearing' is mostly your brain's best current model of what's out there, corrected continuously by incoming data when the model fails. The experience of reality is, in significant part, a sophisticated controlled hallucination running in real time.</p><p>This matters for cyberdelics because it means VR doesn't need to fool the brain perfectly. It needs to generate predictions the brain can't easily correct.</p>

*Trigger:* Predictive Processing Defined

---

### Section: Predictive Processing Defined

<p>Predictive Processing, developed most fully by neuroscientist Karl Friston and philosopher Andy Clark, proposes that the brain operates as a hierarchical prediction machine. At every level, from basic sensory processing to high-level cognition, the brain generates predictions about incoming input and updates its model only when prediction errors are significant enough to warrant revision.</p><p>The key term is prediction error. the signal generated when incoming data doesn't match what the brain anticipated. These errors are what actually drive perception and learning. Most of the time, the brain minimizes prediction error by either updating its model or acting on the world to make the world match the model.</p><p>This framework explains an enormous range of phenomena, from basic sensory illusions to hallucinations, anxiety disorders, and now, how VR can produce altered states.</p>

*Trigger:* How VR Exploits Predictive Processing

---

### Section: How VR Exploits Predictive Processing

**Image Location:** ../assets/images/module3/vr_prediction.webp

<p>High-quality immersive VR works, from a Predictive Processing standpoint, by generating systematic prediction errors that the brain resolves by updating its model rather than by acting to correct the environment.</p><p>When someone puts on a headset and enters a well-designed VR space, their visual system generates predictions about what moving their head should produce. Those predictions are met; the environment responds correctly to movement. The brain updates. this is where I am, this is real. That rapid model update is the mechanism behind the sense of presence.</p><p>Cyberdelic VR goes further by introducing novel coherent environments that generate prediction errors at a higher level, environments the person has no prior model for. Without an established model to fall back on, the brain is forced into genuine updating. This is one explanation for why novel VR environments can produce states of expanded attention and awareness that feel qualitatively different from ordinary perception.</p>

*Trigger:* Embodied Cognition

---

### Section: Embodied Cognition

**Image Location:** ../assets/images/module3/embodied_avatar.webp

<p>Embodied Cognition, developed through the work of Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch, challenges the assumption that cognition happens in the brain alone. Their claim, supported by a substantial body of research, is that cognition is shaped by the whole body's interaction with its environment; not just the neural substrate, but the body's shape, its sensorimotor history, and the way it moves through and acts on the world.</p><p>The practical implication. what kind of body a person inhabits during a VR session affects how they think and feel within it. This isn't just metaphor. Stanford's Virtual Human Interaction Lab has replicated findings showing that embodying a virtual body with specific characteristics, such as different height, different age, or different physical condition, produces measurable changes in attitudes, emotional responses, and self-perception during and after the session.</p><p>The body you put someone into is a design decision with cognitive and therapeutic consequences.</p>

*Trigger:* What Practitioners Do With These Frameworks

---

### Section: What Practitioners Do With These Frameworks

<p>Predictive Processing gives practitioners a clear rationale for why session novelty matters. A client who re-enters the same familiar VR environment session after session will generate fewer prediction errors over time, and the altered state effects will likely diminish. Novel design, rotated environments, and meaningfully varied experiences maintain the conditions for genuine perceptual updating.</p><p>Embodied Cognition gives practitioners a rationale for treating avatar and body representation as therapeutic variables. For a client working on self-compassion, what kind of body do you put them in? For someone processing grief, does first-person perspective serve better than a more expanded or energetic body representation?</p><p>These aren't rhetorical questions. They're session design decisions with theoretical backing.</p>
